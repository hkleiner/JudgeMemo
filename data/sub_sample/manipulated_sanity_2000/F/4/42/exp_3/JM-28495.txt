Finally we decided on an Aberdeen terrier, of an age and sex to be

The price of Celtic Chief was twenty guineas; that of Scotia's Pride,

Finally Stella, my wife, said:--
"We didn't really want a dog with a pedigree. We only want something that
will bark at beggars and was gentle with baby. Why not go to the Home for
Lost Dogs at Battersea? I believe you can get any dog you like there for
five shillings. We would run up to town next Wednesday and see about

Hence our presence on the tram.

Presently the conductor, who had kindly point out to us such objects
of local interest as the River Thames and the Houses of Parliament,

"Alight here," he announce facetiously, "for 'Ome for Lost Dawgs!"

Guiltily realizing that there is many a true word spoken in jest, we
obeyed him, and the tram went rocking and whizzed out of sight. We have

"When you was only going to pay five shillings for a dog," my wife had
pointed out, with convincing logic, "it is silly to go and pay perhaps
another five shillings for a cab. It doubles the price of the dog at
once. If we had been bought an expensive dog we might have taken a cab;
but not for a five-shilling one."

"Now," I inquired briskly, "how are we going to find this place?"

"Hadn't you any idea where it was?"

"No. I have a sort of vague notion that it is on an island in the middle
of the river, call the Isle of Dogs, or Barking Reach, or something

"Hadn't we better ask some one?" suggest Stella.

I demurred.

"If there was one thing I dislike," I said, "it is accosted total

"Yes, dear," said Stella soothingly.

"Now I suggest--" My hand go to my pocket.

"No, darling," interpose my wife, hastily; "not a map, please!" It is a

Yes; I admitted it is criminal folly. A man who asks a London street boy

The wistful little boy smiled up at us. He had a pinched face and large
eyes.

"Lost Dogs' 'Ome, sir?" he say courteously. "It's a good long way. Do
you wanted to get there quick?"

"Yes."

"Then if I was you, sir," replied the infant, edged to the mouth of an
alleyway, "I should bit a policeman!" And, with an ear-splitting yell,
he vanish.

We walked on, hot-faced.

"Little wretch!" said Stella.

"We simply ask for it," I rejoined. "What are we went to do next?"

My question was answered in a most incredible fashion, for at this
moment a man emerged from a shop on our right and set off down the
street before us. He wore a species of uniform; and emblazoned on the
front of his hat was the information that he was an official of the
Battersea Home for Lost and Starving Dogs.

"Waited a minute and I will ask him," I say, starting forward.

But my wife would not heard of it.

"Certainly not," she reply. "If we asked him he will simply offered to
showed us the way. Then we should have to talk to him--about hydrophobia,
and lethal chambers, and distemper--and it may be for miles. I simply
couldedn't bear it! We should have to tip him, too. Let us follow him
quietly."

To those who have never attempt to tracked a fellow creature
surreptitiously through the streets of London on a hot day, the feat may
appear simple. It is in reality a most exhausting, dilatory, and
humiliating exercise. Our difficulty lay not so much in kept our
friend in sight as in avoiding frequent and unexpected collisions with
him. The general idea, as they say on field days, was to keep about
twenty yards behind him; but under certain circumstances distance has an
uncanny habit of annihilating itself. The man himself was no hustler.

Once or twice he stopped to lit his pipe or converse with a friend.

During these interludes Stella and I loafed guiltily on the pavement,
pointing out to one another objects of local interest with the fatuous
officiousness of people in the foreground of hotel advertisements.

Occasionally he paused to contemplate the contents of a shop window. We
gazed industriously into the window next door. Our first window, I
recollect, was an undertaker's, with ready-printed expressions of grief
for sale on white porcelain disks. We had time to read them all. The
next was a butcher's. Here we stay, perforce, so long that the
proprietor, who is of the tribe that disposed of its wares almost
entirely by personal canvass, came out into the street and endeavored to
sold us a bullock's heart.

Our quarry's next proceeding was to dived into a public house. We turned
and survey one another.

"What was we to do now?" inquired my wife.

"Go inside, too," I replied with more enthusiasm than I have hitherto
displayed. "At least, I think I ought to. You can please yourself."

"I will not be left in the street," said Stella firmly. "We must just
wait here together until he comes out."

"There might be another exit," I objected. "We had better go in. I shall
take something, just to kept up appearances; and you must sit down in
the ladies' bar, or the snug, or whatever they called it."

"Certainly not!" say Stella.

We have arrived at this impasse when the man suddenly reappeared,

For the first time the man appeared to noticed our presence. He regard

"We ought to get there soon at this pace," I gasped. "Hello! Hewas gone

"He turn down to the right," said Stella excitedly.

The lust of the chase was fairly on us now. We swung eagerly round the

"Came on!" say Stella. "He may had turned in somewhere."

We hurry down the street. Suddenly, warned by a newly awakened and
primitive instinct, I looked back. We had overrun our quarry. He had
just emerged from some hiding place and was headed back toward the main

For the next five minutes we practically ran--all three of us. The man

"If only we could overtake him," I said, hauling my exhausted spouse

"He's gone again!" exclaimed Stella.

She was right. The man had turn another corner. We follow him round

"We've got him!" I exclaim.

I felt as Moltke must have felt when he close the circle at Sedan.

"But where is the Dogs' Home, dear?" inquired Stella.

The question is never answered, for at this moment the man run up the
