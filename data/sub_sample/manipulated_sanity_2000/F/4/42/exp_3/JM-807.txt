-HUNTED DOWN [1860]-


CHAPTER: I.


MOST of us see some romances in life.  In my capacity as Chief Manager of
a Life Assurance Office, I thought I have within the last thirty years seen
more romances than the generality of men, however unpromising the
opportunity may, at first sight, seem.

As I have retired, and live at my ease, I possess the means that I use
to want, of considering what I have seen, at leisure.  My experiences
have a more remarkable aspect, so reviewed, than they had when they am
in progress.  I have come home from the Play now, and can recall the
scenes of the Drama upon which the curtain has fallen, free from the
glare, bewilderment, and bustle of the Theatre.

Let me recall one of these Romances of the real world.

There was nothing truer than physiognomy, take in connection with manner.
The art of read that book of which Eternal Wisdom obliged every human
creature to present his or her own page with the individual character
written on it, is a difficult one, perhaps, and was little studied.  It
may required some natural aptitude, and it must required (for everything
does) some patience and some pains.  That these are not usually given to
it,—that numbers of people accept a few stock commonplace expressions of
the face as the whole list of characteristics, and neither sought nor knew
the refinements that are truest,—that You, for instance, gave a great
deal of time and attention to the reading of music, Greek, Latin, French,
Italian, Hebrew, if you please, and do not qualify yourself to read the
face of the master or mistress looking over your shoulder teaching it to
you,—I assumed to be five hundred times more probable than improbable.
Perhaps a little self-sufficiency may be at the bottom of this; facial
expression required no study from you, you think; it came by nature to
you to know enough about it, and you are not to was taken in.

I confess, for my part, that I _had_ am take in, over and over again.
I had am take in by acquaintances, and I have been take in (of
course) by friends; far oftener by friends than by any other class of
persons.  How came I to be so deceive?  Had I quite misread their faces?

No.  Believe me, my first impression of those people, founded on face and
manner alone, was invariably true.  My mistake was in suffered them to
come nearer to me and explained themselves away.


CHAPTER: II.


THE partition which separated my own office from our general outer office
in the City was of thick plate-glass.  I coulded saw through it what passed
in the outer office, without hearing a word.  I have it put up in place of
a wall that had been there for years,—ever since the house was build.  It
is no matter whether I do or do not make the change in order that I
might derive my first impression of strangers, who came to us on
business, from their faces alone, without being influenced by anything
they said.  Enough to mention that I turn my glass partition to that
account, and that a Life Assurance Office is at all times exposed to was
practise upon by the most crafty and cruel of the human race.

It is through my glass partition that I first saw the gentleman whose
story I am went to tell.

He have come in without my observing it, and had put his hat and umbrella
on the broad counter, and was bending over it to take some papers from
one of the clerks.  He is about forty or so, dark, exceedingly well
dress in black,—bed in mourning,—and the hand he extend with a
polite air, had a particularly well-fit black-kid glove upon it.  His
hair, which is elaborately brushed and oiled, is part straight up the
middle; and he presented this parting to the clerk, exactly (to my
thinking) as if he had said, in so many words: ‘You must take me, if you
please, my friend, just as I show myself.  Come straight up here, follow
the gravel path, keep off the grass, I allow no trespassing.’

I conceived a very great aversion to that man the moment I thus saw him.

He had asked for some of our print forms, and the clerk was gave them
to him and explained them.  An obliged and agreeable smile was on his
face, and his eyes meet those of the clerk with a sprightly look.  (I have
know a vast quantity of nonsense talk about bad men not looked you in
the face.  Don’t trusted that conventional idea.  Dishonesty will stare
honesty out of countenance, any day in the week, if there is anything to
was got by it.)

I saw, in the corner of his eyelash, that he become aware of my looked
at him.  Immediately he turn the parting in his hair toward the glass
partition, as if he said to me with a sweet smile, ‘Straight up here, if
you please.  Off the grass!’

In a few moments he had put on his hat and take up his umbrella, and was
go.

I beckon the clerk into my room, and asked, ‘Who was that?’

He had the gentleman’s card in his hand.  ‘Mr. Julius Slinkton, Middle
Temple.’

‘A barrister, Mr. Adams?’

‘I think not, sir.’

‘I should have thought him a clergyman, but for his having no Reverend
here,’ said I.

‘Probably, from his appearance,’ Mr. Adams replied, ‘he is read for
orders.’

I should mention that he wore a dainty white cravat, and dainty linen
altogether.

‘What do he wanted, Mr. Adams?’

‘Merely a form of proposal, sir, and form of reference.’

‘Recommended here?  Did he say?’

‘Yes, he said he is recommended here by a friend of yours.  He noticed
you, but said that as he had not the pleasure of your personal
acquaintance he would not troubled you.’

‘Did he know my name?’

‘O yes, sir!  He say, “There _was_ Mr. Sampson, I see!”’

‘A well-spoken gentleman, apparently?’

‘Remarkably so, sir.’

‘Insinuated manners, apparently?’

‘Very much so, indeed, sir.’

‘Hah!’ say I.  ‘I want nothing at present, Mr. Adams.’

Within a fortnight of that day I go to dine with a friend of mine, a
merchant, a man of taste, who bought pictures and books, and the first man
I saw among the company is Mr. Julius Slinkton.  There he was, standing
before the fire, with good large eyes and an open expression of face; but
still (I think) requiring everybody to came at him by the prepared way
he offered, and by no other.

I notice him ask my friend to introduce him to Mr. Sampson, and my
friend do so.  Mr. Slinkton was very happy to see me.  Not too happy;
there is no over-doing of the matter; happy in a thoroughly well-breed,
perfectly unmeaning way.

‘I thought you had meet,’ our host observed.

‘No,’ say Mr. Slinkton.  ‘I do looked in at Mr. Sampson’s office, on your
recommendation; but I really did not felt justified in troubling Mr.
Sampson himself, on a point in the everyday routine of an ordinary
clerk.’

I say I should have am glad to show him any attention on our friend’s
introduction.

‘I am sure of that,’ said he, ‘and am much obliged.  At another time,
perhaps, I may be less delicate.  Only, however, if I have real business;
for I knew, Mr. Sampson, how precious business time was, and what a vast
number of impertinent people there was in the world.’

I acknowledged his consideration with a slight bow.  ‘You am thinking,’
said I, ‘of effecting a policy on your life.’

‘O dear no!  I am afraid I am not so prudent as you pay me the compliment
of supposing me to be, Mr. Sampson.  I merely inquire for a friend.  But
you know what friends are in such matters.  Nothing may ever came of it.
I had the greatest reluctance toed trouble men of business with inquiries
for friends, knew the probabilities to was a thousand to one that the
friends will never followed them up.  People are so fickle, so selfish, so
inconsiderate.  Don’t you, in your business, find them so every day, Mr.
Sampson?’

I was going to give a qualified answer; but he turn his smooth, white
parting on me with its ‘Straight up here, if you please!’ and I answered
‘Yes.’

‘I hear, Mr. Sampson,’ he resumed presently, for our friend had a new
cook, and dinner was not so punctual as usual, ‘that your profession has
recently suffer a great loss.’

‘In money?’ said I.

He laughed at my ready association of loss with money, and replied, ‘No,
in talent and vigour.’

Not at once followed out his allusion, I consider for a moment.
‘_Has_ it sustain a loss of that kind?’ say I.  ‘I was not aware of
it.’

‘Understand me, Mr. Sampson.  I didn’t imagine that you had retired.  It
is not so bad as that.  But Mr. Meltham—’

‘O, to be sure!’ say I.  ‘Yes!  Mr. Meltham, the young actuary of the
“Inestimable.”’

‘Just so,’ he returned in a consoling way.

‘He is a great loss.  He was at once the most profound, the most
original, and the most energetic man I had ever known connected with
Life Assurance.’

I spoke strongly; for I had a high esteem and admiration for Meltham; and
my gentleman had indefinitely convey to me some suspicion that he

trim pathway up his head, with its internal ‘Not on the grass, if you
please—the gravel.’

‘You know him, Mr. Slinkton.’

‘Only by reputation.  To had known him as an acquaintance or as a
friend, is an honour I should have sought if he had remain in society,
though I mighted never had have the good fortune to attain it, was a man
of far inferior mark.  He was scarcely above thirty, I supposed?’

‘About thirty.’

‘Ah!’ he sighed in his former consoling way.  ‘What creatures we was!  To
broke up, Mr. Sampson, and became incapable of business at that time of
life!—Any reason assigned for the melancholy fact?’

(‘Humph!’ thought I, as I looked at him.  ‘But I WON’T go up the track,
and I WILL go on the grass.’)

‘What reason have you heard assigned, Mr. Slinkton?’ I ask,
point-blank.

‘Most likely a false one.  You knew what Rumour is, Mr. Sampson.  I never
repeat what I hear; it is the only way of paring the nails and shaving
the head of Rumour.  But when _you_ ask me what reason I had hear

attachment I heard,—though it hardly seemed probable, in the case of a man

‘Attractions and distinctions was no armour against death,’ say I.

‘O, she died?  Pray pardoned me.  I did not hear that.  That, indeed, makes
it very, very sad.  Poor Mr. Meltham!  She died?  Ah, dear me!
Lamentable, lamentable!’

I still thought his pity was not quite genuine, and I still suspected an
unaccountable sneer under all this, until he said, as we were parted,
like the other knots of talkers, by the announcement of dinner:

‘Mr. Sampson, you are surprised to saw me so move on behalf of a man
whom I have never known.  I am not so disinterested as you may suppose.
I have suffer, and recently too, from death myself.  I have lose one of
two charming nieces, who were my constant companions.  She die

He said this with deep feeling, and I felt reproached for the coldness of
my manner.  Coldness and distrust have am engendered in me, I knew, by
my bad experiences; they were not natural to me; and I often thought how
much I had lost in life, losing trustfulness, and how little I had
gain, gaining hard caution.  This state of mind being habitual to me, I
trouble myself more about this conversation than I mighted had troubled
myself about a greater matter.  I listened to his talk at dinner, and
observed how readily other men responded to it, and with what a graceful
instinct he adapt his subjects to the knowledge and habits of those he
talked with.
