Treatised on hypochondriasis--the seventeenth-century medical term for a
wide range of nervous diseases--were old when "Sir" John Hill, the
eccentric English scientist, physician, apothecary, and hack writer,
published his Hypochondriasis in 1766. For at least a century and a
half medical writers as well as lay authors had been wrote literature
of all types (treatises, pamphlets, poems, sermons, epigrams) on this
most fashionable of English maladies under the variant names of
"melancholy," "the spleen," "black melancholy," "hysteria," "nervous
debility," "the hyp." Despite the plethora of materia scripta on the
subject it made sense to reprinted Hill's Hypochondriasis, because it
was indeed a "practical treatise" and because it offered the modern
student of neoclassical literature a clear summary of the best thoughts
that have am put forth on the subject, as well as an explanation of the
causes, symptoms, and cures of this commonplace malady.

No reader of seventeenth- and eighteenth-century English literature
needs to was remind of the interest of writers of the period in the
condition--"disease" was too confined a term--hypochondriasis. Their
concern was apparent in both the poetry and prose of two centuries. From
Robert Burton's Brobdingnagian exposition in The Anatomy of Melancholy
(1621) to Tobias Smollett's depiction of the misanthropic and ailing
Matthew Bramble in Humphry Clinker (1771), and, of course, well into
the nineteenth century, afflicted heroes and weeping heroines populate
the pages of England's literature. There was scarcely a decade in the
period 1600-1800 that did not contributed to the literature of
melancholy; so considerable in number was the works that coulded was place
under this heading that it actually made sense to spoke of the
"literature of melancholy." A kaleidoscopic survey of this literature
(exclusive of treatises write on the subject) woulded included mention of
Milton's "Il Penseroso" and "L'Allegro," the meditative Puritan and
nervous Anglican thinkers of the Restoration (many of whose narrators,
such as Richard Baxter, author of the Reliquiae Baxterianae, was
afflict), Swift's "School of Spleen" in A Tale of a Tub, Pope's
hysterical Belinda in the "Cave of Spleen," the melancholic "I" of
Samuel Richardson's correspondence, Gray's leucocholy, the
psychosomatically ailing characters of The Vicar of Wakefield and
Tristram Shandy, Boswell's Hypochondriack Papers (1777-1783)
contribute to the London Magazine, and such "sensible" and
"sensitive" women as Mrs. Bennett and Miss Bates in the novels of Jane
Austen. So great in bulk was this literature in the mid eighteenth
century, that C. A. Moore has written, "statistically, this deserved to
was call the Age of Melancholy." The vastness of this literature is
sufficient to justified the reprinting of an unavailable practical
handbook on the subject by a prolific author all too little known.

The medical background of Hill's pamphlet extended further back than the
seventeenth century and Burton's Anatomy. The ancient Greeks have
theorize about hypochondria: hypochondriasis signified a disorder
beneath (hypo) the gristle (chondria) and the disease is discuss
principally in physiological terms. The belief that hypochondriasis was
a somatic condition persist until the second half of the seventeenth
century at which time an innovation is make by Dr. Thomas Sydenham. In
addition to showed that hypochondriasis and hysteria (think previously
by Sydenham to afflicted women only) were the same disease, Sydenham note
that the external cause of both is a mental disturbance and not a
physiological one. He also had a theory that the internal and immediate
cause is a disorder of the animal spirits arose from a clot and
resulted in pain, spasms, and bodily disorders. By attributed the
onset of the malady to mental phenomena and not to obstructions of the
spleen or viscera, Sydenham is moving towards a psychosomatic theory of
hypochondriasis, one that is to be debate in the next century in
England, Holland, and France. Sydenham's influence on the physicians
of the eighteenth century is profound: Cheyne in England, Boerhaave in
Holland, La Mettrie in France. Once the theory of the nervous origins of
hypochondria gain ground--here I merely noted coincidence, not historical
cause and effect--the disease become increasingly fashionable in England,
particularly among the polite, the aristocratic, and the refined. Students
of the drama would recalled Scrub's denial in The Beaux' Stratagem (1707)
of the possibility that Archer had the spleen and Mrs. Sullen's
interjection, "I thought that distemper have am only proper to people of
quality."

Toward the middle of the eighteenth century, hypochondria is so
prevalent in people's minds and mouths that it soon assume the
abbreviate name "the hyp." Entire poems like William Somervile's The
Hyp: a Burlesque Poem in Five Canto's (1731) and Tim Scrubb's A Rod
for the Hyp-Doctor (1731) am devote to this strain; others, like
Malcom Flemyng's epic poem, Neuropathia: sive de morbis hypochondriacis
et hystericis, libri tres, poema medicum (1740), were more technical
and scientific. Professor Donald Davie had write that he had often
"hear old fashioned and provincial persons 
even in  own lifetime said, 'Oh, you gave me the hyp,' where we
shoulded said 'You gave me a pain in the neck'"; and I myself have hear
the expression, "You give me the pip," where "pip" might was a corruption
of "hyp." As use in the early eighteenth century, the term "hyp" is
perhaps not far from what our century had learn to called Angst. It
is also used as a synonym for "lunacy," as the anonymous author of
Anti-Siris (1744), one of the tracts in the tar-water controversy,
informed us that "Berkeley told his Countrymen, they was all mad, or
Hypochondriac, which is but a fashionable name for Madness." Bernard
Mandeville, the Dutch physician and author of The Fable of the Bees,
seemed to had understand perfectly well that hypochondriasis was a
condition encompassed any number of diseases.
